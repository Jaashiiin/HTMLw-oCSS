What is Labor Day?  Labor Day in the Philippines, also known as "Araw ng mga Manggagawa" is a day dedicated to honoring the hard work of Filipino workers. It's a time to recognize their contributions to the nation's economy and society, from the agricultural fields to the bustling urban centers. Labor Day also highlights the ongoing struggle for workers' rights and the need for solidarity among workers across different sectors serving as a platform to advocate for better labor standards and policies that prioritize the welfare of workers. It is marked by various activities and events organized by labor unions, government agencies, and civil society groups. These include parades, rallies, and cultural performances that celebrate the achievements and struggles of Filipino workers. It's also a time for workers to come together in solidarity and demand for their rights, whether through peaceful demonstrations or advocacy campaigns.

History PH Labor day has a rich history dating back to its first celebration on May 1, 1903. Organized by the country's inaugural labor union, "Union Obrera Democratica de Filipina (U.O.D.F.)," over 100,000 workers marched from Tondo's Plaza Moriones to Malacanang, demanding fair wages and improved working conditions under the American-led government. The U.O.D.F., established in February 1902 by Isabelo Delos Reyes and Herminigildo Cruz, aimed to defend the rights of the working class during American occupation. Following Delos Reyes's arrest for rebellion and sedition, Dominador Gomez assumed leadership and continued the advocacy, culminating in the historic 1903 protest. Despite challenges and subsequent arrests, the movement persisted, leading to significant legislative changes. On April 8, 1908, the Philippine Assembly passed a bill declaring May 1st as Labor Day, formally recognizing the contributions and struggles of workers. The inaugural Labor Day celebration in 1913, organized by Herminigildo Cruz, marked the beginning of annual demands for fair labor practices, including the advocacy for an eight-hour workday, an end to child labor, equal standards for women workers, and employer accountability.
